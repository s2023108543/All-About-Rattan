All About Rattan

Rattan is a flowering plant in the palm family, mainly found in Southeast Asia and other parts of Africa. Its resilience, portability, and waterproofness make it extremely valuable. It is widely used in making furniture, handicrafts, and even in construction. Thanks to its strength and ability to withstand various weather conditions, rattan is a great choice for outdoor furniture like dining chairs, sofas, and lounge chairs.

Where does rattan came from?

Southeast Asia is home to a wide range of rattan species, which are primarily tropical in nature. The major rattan-producing nations are Thailand, Malaysia, the Philippines, and Indonesia where almost 70% of the world's supply comes from Indonesia alone, making it the primary supplier. In the extensive tropical woods of these nations, rattan, a climbing palm, is widely distributed. Due to its ideal growing circumstances and traditional farming methods, Southeast Asia continues to be the primary region for rattan production, despite the fact that rattan is also found in some parts of Africa and Australasia.

How is rattan furniture made?

Harvesting the rattan cane from the forests is the first step in creating rattan furniture. The canes are cleaned and dried after harvest. In order to make "cane," the outer skin, or rind, is frequently peeled off, while the inner core is utilized for a variety of reasons. To increase their endurance and guard against insect damage, the canes are occasionally cooked in oil or treated with chemicals. Depending on the furniture design, the processed rattan canes are either used whole or divided into smaller strips. After that, these parts are sculpted and bent, frequently with the help of steam to increase their pliability. For furniture frames, artisans mold the rattan into the appropriate forms using a variety of techniques. The formed pieces are put together using screws, nails, or binding materials like leather or twine to create furniture components like chairs, tables, and couches. The furniture is then painted or stained to get the desired finish after any sharp edges are smoothed out with sanding. For added longevity and a glossy appearance, a protective coating is frequently applied.

Rattan Crafts

Beyond furniture, rattan is also used to make a wide range of beautiful and functional crafts. Items such as baskets, lampshades, and storage containers can all be crafted from rattan. These handmade crafts add a unique, rustic touch to any home or space, highlighting the natural beauty and flexibility of the material. Rattan crafts are often used to create eco-friendly, one-of-a-kind home décor.

Manau Rattan (Calamus Manan)

Calamus manan, the manau rattan or rotan manau, is a species of flowering plant in the palm family Arecaceae, native to Thailand, Peninsular Malaysia, Sumatra, and Borneo. A vine, its single stem is widely harvested from the wild for cane furniture-making, leading to an unsustainable population decline. One unbranched stem at Buitenzorg (now Bogor Botanical Gardens), was carefully measured to a length of 787 feet (240 meters).

Tohiti Rattan (Calamus Tahitiensis)

Tohiti rattan is known for its quality and smooth texture. This type of rattan is often used to make luxury and decorative items. The main advantage of Tohiti rattan is its very fine texture, making it ideal for products with intricate details and neat finishing. This rattan is also easy to color, so it can be adapted to various interior themes and styles.

Rattan Stem (Calamus Rotang)

Calamus rotang, also known as common rattan, is a plant species native to India, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar (Burma). It is one of the scandent (climbing) rattan palms used to make Malacca cane furniture, baskets, walking sticks, umbrellas, tables, and general wickerwork, and is found in Southwest Asia.

Pulut Rattan (Daemonorops Draco)

Pulut rattan is known for its fine fiber, often used to make ropes and woven items. The advantage of Pulut rattan lies in the very strong strength of its fibers, suitable for making ropes that require durability. This rattan fiber is also very flexible and easy to weave, making it a good choice for handicraft products. Pulut rattan is also resistant to moisture, making it ideal for items used in wet or damp environments.

Kubu Rattan (Calamus Kubu)

Kubu Rattan has a rougher texture and a natural brownish gray color, giving a natural and rustic appearance to the final product. The advantage of Kubu rattan lies in its strength and durability, making it ideal for outdoor furniture. Daemonorops Grandis

"Rotan sendang" is a plant of dryland - generally of steep slopes in the foothills to about 900 m altitude it is found in Malaya and Thailand. At higher altitudes populations often consist of a plethora of forms, of different sizes, varying from very slender (about 1.5 cm in diameter) to very robust; yet the range of intermediates allows the extremes to be identified as D. grandis.

Calamus Scipionum

A vigorous, large, clustering Rattan, widely distributed in lowland rainforests and secondary forests to 500 m (1600 ft.) from the Malay Peninsula to Sumatra, Borneo, and the Philippines. The slender stems are about 5 cm (2 in.) in diameter including the leaf sheaths and can reach a length of more than 50 m (170 ft.), with internodes of more than 1 m (3 ft.). The palm climbs into large trees with flagella, spiny, whip-like organs of the leaf sheath, that can be more than 7 m (23 ft.) long. The elegantly arching leaves hold numerous, broad, lax, regularly arranged leaflets to 40 cm (16 in.) long and do not have cirri. The long inflorescences produce bunches of greenish- or yellowish-white, scaly fruits.

Calamus Zollingeri

Calamus Zollingeri is a spiny, evergreen, climbing palm producing a cluster of vigorous, unbranched stems that can be 40 meters or more long and 25 - 40mm wide. An excellent quality stem, though inferior to Calamus manan. It is commonly harvested from the wild for local use in manting furniture, and is also traded both locally and more widely. The plant has excellent potential for cultivation, especially since it forms a cluster of stems.

Daemonorops Micracantha

Daemonorops Micracantha is a vigorous, climbing, evergreen palm that can produce a cluster of unbranched stems up to 40 meters long. These stems can grow to the very tops of the trees in the rainforest. The plant has a range of uses, being harvested from the wild for mainly local use as a source of material for basketry and a resin with medicinal applications. This is one of several species supplying the resin, which is sold in local markets and sometimes also traded internationally.

Calamus Tenuis

Calamus tenuis is a species of flowering plant in the family Arecaceae. It is native to India, Assam, Bangladesh, Bhutan, Cambodia, Laos, Burma, Thailand, Vietnam, Java and Sumatra. Its natural habitats are subtropical or tropical moist lowland forests and subtropical or tropical moist montane forests where it is threatened by habitat loss.
